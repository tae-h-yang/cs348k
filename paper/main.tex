\begin{abstract}
Kinematic humanoid motion generators such as MotionBricks produce diverse
Unitree G1 reference trajectories, but a kinematically plausible reference is
not necessarily executable by a physics-based controller. We study this gap as
an acceptance and curation problem. Given a fixed MotionBricks candidate pool,
we compute pre-controller diagnostics, evaluate selected references with the
native SONIC G1 tracking stack, train a trajectory-level qpos acceptance model
from native rollout labels, and audit rendered videos frame-by-frame from MP4
pixels. On a broad 13-mode, 104-identity candidate pool, a hand-coded gated
pre-controller selector improves strict native SONIC pass rate from 70/104 for
the deterministic baseline to 78/104. A learned temporal qpos classifier
predicts strict native acceptance better than scalar diagnostics in grouped
cross-validation (AUC 0.864 versus 0.782 for the best scalar), and its
prospective all-candidate selections reach 76/104 strict passes. The learned
selector improves over the deterministic baseline, but does not beat the
hand-coded gate. Its strongest behavior is abstention: thresholding the learned
score at 0.5 removes all 16 unsupported crawling identities and keeps 76 strict
passes over 88 accepted identities. A follow-up hybrid queue closes the missing
native labels and reaches 74/88 strict accepted identities; all 9 newly
evaluated hybrid videos pass visual pixel audit, but two idle clips fail strict
root-drift criteria. The final claim is therefore not that MotionBricks is
fine-tuned or physically solved. The contribution is an honest systems study:
native controller labels and visual audits expose failure modes, hand-coded
gates are currently strongest, and learned acceptance is useful for triage and
abstention before expensive controller rollout.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

MotionBricks is a fast kinematic motion generator with modular latent motion
primitives and real-time controllability \cite{motionbricks}. Its physical
awareness limitation is exactly the problem a robotics user sees in practice:
a generated reference can look plausible in pose space yet exceed controller,
contact, root-height, or balance limits. Recent humanoid work increasingly
treats tracking policies and physical feedback as the arbiter of motion quality:
SONIC scales humanoid motion tracking with large motion data and compute
\cite{sonic}; GMT and SMAP study broad whole-body tracking/adaptation
\cite{gmt,smap}; RLPF uses physical feedback to align large motion models with
humanoid control \cite{rlpf}. Our project asks a smaller question that can be
tested locally: can we curate a fixed MotionBricks candidate pool into more
controller-executable references, and can we tell when the selector should
abstain?

This framing deliberately avoids an overclaim. We do not fine-tune
MotionBricks. We do not train a new tracking policy. We do not prove real-robot
safety. Instead, we make the generator/control gap measurable with native
SONIC rollouts, learned acceptance prediction, hard gates, and video audits.

\section{Pipeline}

\paragraph{Candidate pool.}
The final broad run uses 13 exposed MotionBricks G1 modes, 8 seeds, and
stochastic candidate sampling, producing 832 candidates over 104
\((\mathrm{mode}, \mathrm{seed})\) identities. The modes include idle, upright
walking/style variants, and hand/elbow crawling stress tests. Each candidate is
exported as a Unitree G1 qpos trajectory and round-tripped to SONIC reference
CSVs; the export audit reports max qpos error \(3.33\times 10^{-16}\), so the
main failures are not explained by a broad joint-order conversion bug.

\paragraph{Pre-controller diagnostics.}
For every candidate we compute inverse-dynamics demand, root-height sanity,
contact artifacts, self-contact, non-foot floor contact, support proxies, foot
skate, velocity, acceleration, and jerk. These are not final physical proof:
they are cheap pre-controller triage features.

\paragraph{Native controller labels.}
Selected references are run through the native SONIC G1 release protocol with
the robot released before tracking. A rollout is counted as \emph{strict} if it
does not fall, has mean joint RMSE \(\leq 0.20\) rad, and has mean root XY error
\(\leq 1.5\) m. We report survival separately from strict pass because several
clips survive but drift too far.

\paragraph{Learned acceptance model.}
A temporal qpos CNN is trained on 405 native rollout labels from the broad run.
Splits are grouped by identity so duplicate selector choices from the same
mode/seed do not leak across folds. The model predicts strict native acceptance
from the reference trajectory alone; it is an acceptance model, not a generator.

\paragraph{Visual audit.}
Numbers are not enough for motion. We rerender native rollouts with camera
tracking and contact markers, then run a frame-level MP4 audit that segments
the red actual robot and white reference robot in every frame. The audit flags
missing bodies, large visual separation, fallen-looking body aspect, and sudden
visual jumps. We use this as a systematic review aid, not as a replacement for
human validation.

main identity-level comparison is:

\subsection{Learned acceptance predicts better than scalar diagnostics}

The temporal qpos acceptance model reaches grouped cross-validated AUC 0.864
and average precision 0.917. The best scalar diagnostic is root-z minimum at
AUC 0.782; pre-controller score reaches AUC 0.752. This supports the idea that
native acceptability is trajectory-structured, not fully captured by a single
root/contact/risk scalar.

\subsection{Learned selection improves over baseline but not over the gate}

We score all 832 broad13 candidates with the learned model and export the best
candidate per identity. Native SONIC evaluates all 104 learned selections:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Prospective learned-selector native rollout.}
\label{tab:learned}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
Selector & All strict & Upright strict & Idle strict & Crawling strict \\
\midrule
Deterministic baseline & 70/104 & 63/80 & 7/8 & 0/16 \\
Hand-coded gated selector & 78/104 & 71/80 & 7/8 & 0/5 selected \\
Learned acceptance selector & 76/104 & 68/80 & 8/8 & 0/16 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

This is not a clean deployment split: the learned model selects from the same
broad13 candidate pool used to train labels. Of its 104 selected candidates,
46 had already been native-evaluated in the broad selector study, while 58 were
newly evaluated here. The newly evaluated subset is 37/58 strict, including
36/42 upright strict and 0/15 newly evaluated crawling. Thus the learned
selector is promising, but not a new state-of-the-art result.

\subsection{Abstention is the strongest learned behavior}

The learned score strongly separates unsupported crawling in this run. A score
threshold of 0.5 removes all 16 crawling identities and keeps all 76 strict
passes:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Learned-score abstention on the 104 learned selections.}
\label{tab:abstain}
\begin{tabular}{rrrrr}
\toprule
Min score & Accepted identities & Strict & Falls & Crawling accepted \\
\midrule
0.00 & 104 & 76/104 & 22 & 16 \\
0.50 & 88 & 76/88 & 6 & 0 \\
0.80 & 88 & 76/88 & 6 & 0 \\
0.85 & 77 & 65/77 & 6 & 0 \\
0.90 & 72 & 62/72 & 6 & 0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We then build a first all-candidate hybrid queue: reject crawling and low-root
references, require the root/contact sanity gate, then rank by learned score.
It accepts 88 identities and rejects 16. After evaluating the 9 selected
references not already covered by the learned rollout, the closed-label hybrid
queue reaches 74/88 strict accepted identities: 68/80 upright and 6/8 idle.
All 9 newly evaluated hybrid videos survive and pass visual audit, but two idle
clips fail strict because root XY drift exceeds 1.5m. This reveals the next
missing feature: idle/root-drift sanity must be part of the selector.

\subsection{Visual audit prevents metric-only overclaiming}

The learned-selector tracked-camera video audit reports 27 visual passes,
61 warnings, and 16 visual failures over 104 videos. The failures are dominated
by crawling collapses and a few upright fall/separation cases. Only one native
strict pass is also a visual fail; we exclude that clip from presentation
material. The 9 newly evaluated hybrid diagnostic videos have 9/9 visual pass.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{What is submittable.}
This is defensible as a course final project if framed as controller-in-the-loop
curation for a fixed kinematic generator. The most robust claim is:
native SONIC exposes a real kinematic-to-dynamic gap; hard gates improve
baseline selection; a learned qpos acceptance model predicts controller success
better than scalar diagnostics and is useful for abstention/triage; visual
audit is necessary to prevent metric-only mistakes.

\paragraph{What is not submittable as a claim.}
The project does not show MotionBricks fine-tuning, guaranteed physical
feasibility, solved crawling, or superiority of the learned selector over the
hand-coded gate. The learned rollout is partly same-pool, and the hybrid queue
is diagnostic rather than a final win.

\paragraph{Next research direction.}
The strongest pivot is not another heuristic. It is physical-feedback training:
use native tracker outcomes and visual/contact failures to update either the
generator distribution or a learned proposer, similar in spirit to
controller-feedback alignment methods \cite{rlpf}. Locally, the next small step
is to add root-drift/idle sanity to the hybrid selector and rerun a fresh
candidate pool. At research scale, the right experiment is to train or adapt a
tracking policy or generator with controller outcomes in the loop.
